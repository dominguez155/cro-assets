§ 01 · The Gap

The industry has estimating software. It doesn't have financial software.

Shops running $1.2M–$3M a year rely on CCC, Mitchell, and Audatex to build estimates — and QuickBooks to record what already happened. Nothing in the stack answers the question that actually matters on Monday morning: what do I do tomorrow? Five pain points define the gap.

01

Accounts Receivable Aging

Deductibles collected late or missed entirely. Insurance checks drift 10–30 days. No clean picture of what's owed at any given moment.

02

Supplement Float

Supplements pending approval represent significant contingent revenue — most shops don't actively track or chase it by carrier.

03

Job Costing Blindness

Parts, labor, and sublet costs are tracked in silos. Rarely matched against individual RO revenue in real time.

04

Cash Timing Mismatches

Parts invoices and payroll hit weekly. Insurance payments arrive 10–30 days post-delivery. The timing gap is where cash quietly disappears.

05

Budgets Without Data

Monthly budgets built on estimate volume rather than actual collection schedules. Decisions made against numbers that never existed.

→

The Throughline

These aren't management failures. They're information failures — and information is a solvable problem.

Field Insight

A shop doing $200K/month in estimates may only collect $160K in the same window. The $40K gap — uncollected deductibles, pending supplements, delayed carrier checks — is recoverable cash. The goal of this work is teaching managers to find it, track it, and get it.

§ 04 · The Thesis

The next decade belongs to the shops that treat data as infrastructure.

For thirty years, the collision industry has been told that running a good shop means fixing cars well. It does — but that's the floor, not the ceiling.

The shops that will define the next decade are the ones that treat their financial data the way a good technician treats a damaged frame: something to measure precisely, align deliberately, and return to spec. The instinct is already there on the floor. What's missing is the instrumentation.

Estimating platforms tell you what a job should cost. Accounting software tells you what already happened. Neither tells a manager what to do tomorrow. That gap — between the estimate and the ledger — is where shops have been losing money quietly for as long as anyone can remember.

What's coming next is not more information. The industry has plenty of information. What's coming is instrumentation: browser-native tools that turn paint hours into projections, repair orders into cash schedules, supplements into pipelines. Tools a manager can open on a Monday morning and use to make Tuesday better.

That's the work. That's what we're building toward.
